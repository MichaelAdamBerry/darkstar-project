GRATEFUL DATA

What 30 Years of Set List Data Can Tell Us About the Grateful Dead

Created By Adam Berry

Tweet

Even for die hard deadheads The Grateful Dead can be difficult to categorize. For the uninitiated, the sheer volume of recordings and songs is enough to be scared away. As music critic Tim Sommer sums up nicely--

"Loving the Grateful Dead is like ridin’ that train to Hogwarts—- you must believe that the track exists in order to climb aboard."

These visualizations use data from archive.com and setlist.fm to examine what songs were played most, the size of the repetoir, and the lifespan of each song.

Most Played Songs

This radial bar chart shows the top ten most played songs by the Grateful Dead, excluding instrumentals Drums and Space which were played at every show from the early 80's onward. The length of each arc represents the amount of times played with the highest value at the outermost position and the lowest value at the innermost position.

Observations

All songs that made the top ten list debuted within the first seven years of the Dead's career - six from the 60's, 2 from 1971, and 2 from 1972

The 1960's gave us four covers -- Good Lovin'('65), I Know You Rider('65), Not Fade Away('68) and Me and My Uncle ('67') as well as some of the earliest original songs -- The Other One('67), and China Cat Sunflower('68)

1970 saw fan favorites Sugar Magnolia and Truckin' debut

1971 was the last year for a top-ten song to debut and the birthyear of Jack Straw and the most played song Playin' in the Band

As the Dead introduced new songs into their performances. Setlists became more varied.

All Songs Scaled by Times Played

This chart displays the incredible volume of the Grateful Dead's Career repetoir. Spheres represent every unique song played with the diameter sized by number of times played.

Select any song from either chart to see the songs performance history, indicating the debut year, peak year, and last year played. All of the bands most played songs peaked in the early 70's. This indicates sets tended to include fewer deviations in songs from show to show, but later, as the bands repetoir expanded, so did the amount of unique songs that were played in a given year.

**Note on data from 1965-1967 - Many shows and setlists from the earliest years of the bands existence is incomplete. Data can be trusted for accuracy from 1968 onward.
